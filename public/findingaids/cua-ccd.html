Historical Note

The concept of the Confraternity of Christian Doctrine (CCD) began in Italy in the 16th Century in the aftermath of the Council of Trent. It was created for the purpose of giving religious instruction to counteract ignorance or indifference to church teaching. The reform minded St. Charles Borromeo of Milan was a strong proponent. In 1607, Pope Paul V established the Society of Christian Doctrine with headquarters in Rome.

CCD was strongly promoted by Pope Pius X in the early twentieth century, especially with his encyclical letter Acerbo nimis in 1905. Thereafter, parishes and dioceses in America began to organize confraternities because of the need for religious instruction in remote rural areas as well as for immigrants in urban areas such as Los Angeles, New York, and Pittsburgh. However, the CCD only gained official recognition in the United States in 1934 when the National Catholic Rural Life Conference, under the leadership of Bishop Edwin V. O'Hara, featured a 'Confraternity Day' as part of its annual meeting.

Also in 1934, the American bishops, during their annual meeting, created a permanent Episcopal Committee on the CCD under the leadership of Bishop O'Hara. A national center was set up in 1935 and functioned as a bureau under the Executive Department of the National Catholic Welfare Conference (known since 2001 as the United States Conference of Catholic Bishops). A driving force in the center was Rev. Joseph Burns Collins, priest-director from 1942 to 1967. Another was Miriam Marks, who served as executive secretary from 1935 to 1960.

The center was transferred to the Education Department in 1969, and was dissolved in 1974. A new organization, the National Conference of Diocesan Directors (NCDD), operated as a part of the bishops conference, was founded in 1967. In 1982 the NCDD became an independent entity later known as the National Conference for Catechetical Leadership (NCCL).

Scope and Contents

These are the records of the national office, or working secretariat, of the Confraternity of Christian Doctrine (CCD). They complement the records of the Episcopal Committee on CCD and researchers should consider examining these records as well.

Series 1 contains General Correspondence Files, 1935-1974, in boxes 1-13. There are three subseries, all arranged alphabetically. The first, Diocesan, 1935-1971, boxes 1-10, has correspondence with, among others, Baltimore, Kansas City, Los Angeles, the Military Ordinariate, and New York. The second, Foreign, 1935-1974, boxes 10-11, has international correspondence, especially to Africa. The third, Publishers, 1935-1974, in boxes 11-13, is self-explanatory.

Series 2 has Subject Files, 1935-1982, in boxes 14-29. There are two subseries. The first, General, 1935-1974, in boxes 14-26, is filed alphabetically. Of particular note here is material dealing with CCD Congresses, Episcopal Committee, and the National Center. The second, Religious Education files, is located in boxes 26-27 and consists of research into religious education and organizations. The third, Biblical and Catechetical, 1938-1982, is a small subseries located in boxes 28-29 consisting primarily of publications and publicity material.

Series 3 contains Priest-Director Joseph B. Collins Files, 1943-1952, in box 29. Collins was the long-time director of the National CCD Office and these files include his addresses, articles, and books reviews.

Series 4 is Scrapbooks, 1933-1957, in boxes 30-34. There are five subseries. The first is General Publications and Clippings, 1933-1957, in boxes 30-31. The second, National and Regional Congresses, 1936-1955, is in box 32. The third, Diocesan, 1940-1951, is in box 33. The fourth, Public High School Retreats, 1952-1953, and the fifth, Master List of U.S. Colleges, ca. 1955, are located in box 34.

Series 5 is Publications, 1935-1973, in boxes 35-37 and divided into five subseries. They are Printed Proceedings of the National Convention/Congress, 1935-1966; Bibles and Biblical Readings, 1938-1965; Manuals, Lessons, and Workshops, 1941-1966; Lay Training Course Kits, 1950-1953; and Miscellaneous, 1946-1973.

Series 6 contains the Working Files of the National Catechetical Directory Committee, 1973-1977, in boxes 38-39. It is divided into four subseries: Minutes, 1973-1977; Planning Documentation, 1973-1977; Recommendations, 1973-1977; and Drafts, 1973-1977, both published and unpublished.

Series 7 has Photographs and Oversize, 1921-1960, in boxes 40-41. The photographs consist of mounted silver gelatin photo prints with sizes ranging from 2" x 3" to 8" x 10". The photographs record CCD activities, primarily in local parishes. They are arranged by diocese or archdiocese whenever possible. Portraits of national leaders, copies of journal articles, and a correspondence file are included. Phrases identifying photographs in quotation marks are taken from notes on the backs of photographs, captions, or accompanying material. The oversize material in box 41 is a mix of correspondence, photographs, and printed materials, 1942-1946 from San Antonio and Philadelphia.
