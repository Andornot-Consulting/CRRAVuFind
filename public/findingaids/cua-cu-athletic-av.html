Acquisition Information

This collection combines seven separate accessions. In June 1970, the Athletic Department deposited a number of collections of newspaper clippings labeled as "improvised scrapbooks" in the archives, as well as numerous oversized scrapbooks. These scrapbooks spanned the years 1929-1939. The oversized scrapbooks contained clippings that had been mounted on the bound pages of the scrapbooks, they were then wrapped in large protective sheets of paper and bound with string. The improvised scrapbooks consisted of miscellaneous newspaper clippings from the Tower and local papers, which were later mounted on unbound 8 Â½ x 11 plain white paper in 1972 and wrapped in large sheets of paper. These scrapbooks were labeled as including information about football, basketball, and boxing. The Archives housed these scrapbooks in three Hollinger boxes, which were incorporated into this larger athletic collection in 2005.

In August 1974, J. Brian McCall, Director of Athletics, deposited a collection of photographs in the archives. These photographs spanned the dates 1914-1950. One photograph was deposited in the archives in 1982 by coach Joe Fisher.

In 1985, 3.5 linear feet of athletic material was donated by Francis Murray, the Equipment Manager. The material included films, photographs, an insignia book of athletic council with blank certificates, and scorebooks from 1930-1975.

In 1987, one record center box containing annual reports from 1966-1985 and general administrative files from 1953-1985 were donated by Joan C. Kemp, Secretary of the DuFour Center.

Further deposits were not made until 2001. In July an athletic publication from 1930 was donated to the archives by President O'Connell. In September President O'Connell donated two photographs and two photocopied pictures depicting athletic related scenes and announcements from the 1920s.

In 2005, Yvonne McConville, the Athletics Administrative Assistant, deposited 19 linear feet of athletic material in the archives. The material contained a large amount of general administrative files and material on various sports teams. The date range was 1989-2005. This material, in combination with the previous material in the athletic department record group, now comprises the athletic department collection.

In 2008, another survey of the Department of Athletics was performed. In early 2009, 18 linear feet was deposited by Matthew Hannaford, with additional deposits expected.

On May 27, 2010, Jennifer Dowd, daughter of Coach Dowd, donated a DVD that she made using materials from the Department of Athletics Records, Public Affairs Records, The CUA University Photographic Collection, and CUA student publications including the Cardinal Yearbook and The Tower. This DVD was used for an Athletics banquet honoring Coach Marty Dowd.

Another accession of materials was made on July 12, 2011 which included photographs, paper records, and materials from an exhibit. Additional materials arrived in September 2013.

The university records of the Department of Athletics are physically separated from this series.

Historical Note

Students formed small, unofficial, and poorly organized football, baseball, and relay teams soon after the founding of the school in 1889, but a formal Athletic Association was not established at Catholic University until the fall of 1896. The association and campus athletics floundered at the school for several years because of lack of support and the need for adequate facilities. While there was a small gym in the basement of Keane Hall and several tennis and hand ball courts there lacked a full sized athletic field. Baseball was quite popular but it was not until 1905 that a group of dedicated students formed an official team. They soon transformed the empty plot of land near the old observatory into a baseball diamond and became the most popular sports team on campus.

The year 1906 exhibited the enduring desire by students to be represented in the athletic field by the re-organization of an Athletic Association and the election of formal association officials. The association vigilantly argued for a new larger gymnasium. Construction was started four years later to retrofit an existing building for athletics and by 1911 the university was equipped with a slightly larger gymnasium. But the student body badly needed a full size gymnasium and did not curtail its cry for a new gym.

Between 1910 and 1920 the student body increased in large numbers and this saw great expansion in the athletic teams, the football team enjoyed some minor success, the baseball team continued to flourish, and the basketball team quickly expanded, as well as the track and field programs. The year 1910 marked the beginning of a solid and talented baseball team, and in the school year 1912-1913 saw the creation of a formal Athletic Council and the basketball team making their first collegiate record in the number of points scored in one game and winning the South Atlantic Title. In 1915 the baseball coach Charles V. Moran became the first Athletic Director. The new successes and obvious strength of the athletic department assisted in its admittance to the South Atlantic Intercollegiate Athletic Association.

As athletics became increasingly popular among the students the argument for a better equipped gym and swimming facility became stronger. By 1916 the student body was at its biggest with 400 lay students and 200 ecclesiastical students which was strong support for larger athletic facilities. In 1918, the school began fundraising efforts for a new gymnasium. Staff, Alumni and the Knights of Columbus all submitted donations and in 1919 construction began to build a brand new 44,000 square foot gymnasium. By the start of the new year the financing had been gathered and four short years later the gym was officially dedicated on October 4, 1924.

Between the years 1920-1940 the university underwent several changes which effected the athletic program. Rector Thomas J. Shahan further developed the undergraduate program and redefined the mission of the school. The trend to align the school closer to its founding catholic ideals lead to future rectors further emphasizing academics over athletics. Even with less support being given to the athletic teams this period saw the rise of several intramural programs, such as boxing and fencing, and the football team winning the legendary 1936 Orange Bowl championship. In 1941, Rector Joseph M. Corrigan was facing the resignation of two of his head coaches, a military draft caused by World War II, and the pressures of Vatican II, so he began a new policy which de-emphasized athletics.

CUA athletics continued with modest gains over the next few decades. Eddie LaFond, who as a student had a renowned athletic career at CUA in football, basketball, and boxing in the 1920s, had been named as the new Athletic Director in 1941. In his 28 years as athletic director he helped the 1943-1944 men's basketball team win the Mason-Dixon Conference title and make the program's first trip to the NCAA Tournament, while supporting the football team in their bid to the 1940 Sun Bowl.

But it was not until the 1960s and 1970s that sports again played a prominent role in the university. The men's basketball team advanced to the 1963-1964 Division II tournament. The passage of Title IX in 1972 and the creation of the Association for Intercollegiate Athletics for Women (AIAW) influenced the creation of many new teams for women, such as women's basketball, field hockey, tennis, swimming, and track and field teams. In 1977, the football team went to the Division I playoffs and the baseball team advanced to the NCAA Division I tournament. During this time new programs were added to the department, such as soccer, while older programs, such as golf and tennis, flourished.

The 1980s brought many new changes to the athletic department. The first being that the school decided to raise funds for a proposed new multi purpose athletic center, which conflicted with the fundraising needed to support the athletics Division I standing. This conflict, teamed with other financial concerns and the lack of a modern field house, resulted in the university's decision in 1980 to drop from Division I to Division III of the National Collegiate Athletic Association (NCAA). An Athletic Advisory Board was quickly established with the responsibility of developing a set of formal goals and objectives of the athletic department, work with interested alumni, and to promote support for athletics among the student body. It was obvious from the fall out in within the student body resulting from the change in the school's NCAA Division I status that more communication was needed between the administration and the students in regard to major decisions that affected the athletic programs; this advisory board was established to meet that need.

As participation in women's sports teams expanded nationally, changes in the structure and organization of women's sports became necessary. CUA made the decision to align their women's programs with the NCAA after the dissolution of the AIAW. Under the leadership of Jone Dowd, the first Coordinator of Women's Sports, women's teams slowly flourished. Volleyball and cheerleading were added to the women's sports program.

The students finally realized their dream of a modern athletic facility with the opening of the Raymond A. DuFour athletic complex in the fall of 1985. The complex included greatly expanded courts for track and field events, tennis, basketball, an Olympic sized pool, weight-training machines, racquetball courts, as well as other amenities such as a sauna, and dance studio. This facility influenced the burgeoning interest in athletics at CUA with the development of a Lifetime Activities and Leisure Studies program. The program was created to meet the health and fitness goals of students, faculty, and staff of CUA. The program included classes in ballet, jazz, theater dance, tap dance, modern dance, fencing, and karate. It also encouraged the creation of further athletic programs such as ball room dancing, and club sports such as ice hockey, men's and women's rowing and rugby.

The 1990s saw continued success and growth in CUA athletics. The 1995-1996 basketball team won their regular season title in the Capital Athletic Conference (CAC) and received an at-large bid to the 1996 NCAA Division III tournament. The basketball team's victories continued as they won their second CAC title and advanced to the NCAA Division III Tournament for the third time in six seasons in 1997-1998. The football team enjoyed a good season as well in 1997 as they reached the Division III playoffs for the first time with an undefeated regular season.

Throughout the past few years the basketball team has become the most widely recognized team at the university. In 2000-2001, the team won the NCAA Division III national championships, making it the first team national championship in school history. The school continues to take strides that will keep CUA athletics strong. In 2005, Catholic University helped formed a new NCAA Division III athletics conference that will incorporate universities from four mid-Atlantic states and District of Columbia. The women's soccer teams is also doing quite well. In 2005, they won the team's first CAC Championship and earned an NCAA Tournament berth for the first time. The CUA cheerleading team has also risen to national fame and will compete for the Division II/III national title in the University Cheerleading Association College Cheerleading and Dance Team Championship Tournament in 2006. The team will enter the tournament ranked No. 4 in the country.

Scope and Contents

This series is divided in two sub-series: Athletics Photographs, 1889-Present and Athletics Audiovisual Media, 1920-Present. The first sub-series contains 33 boxes of photographs, which include black and white and colored photographs. It also contains negatives, contact sheets, and slides. The second sub-series, however, contains 8 boxes of primarily 16mm film footages in both black and white and color.

The first sub-series is comprised of images about various events in the Department of Athletics from 1889 to present. Events include award ceremonies and sports competitions. Photographs of sports events include CUA's baseball team, women's softball team, men's and women's basketball team, field hockey team, men's and women's lacrosse team, football team, aerobics team, ballroom dancing team, band, cheerleading squad, men's and women's soccer team, men's and women's swim team, cross country team, men's and women's tennis team, volleyball team, Athletic Department staff members, and athletic facilities. In addition, this sub-series includes publication images and profile photos of key team players and head coaches.

The second sub-series, however, contains audiovisual footages of various sports events from 1920 to present. This sub-series primarily consists of 16mm film footages with contents such as football games and track and field meets. Competitions were between Catholic University and other schools such as Georgetown University, Ford University, Mount St. Mary's University and many more.
