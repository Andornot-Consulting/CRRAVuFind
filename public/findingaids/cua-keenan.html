Biographical Note

Joseph Daniel Keenan was a labor leader who was an important labor-government laison during the Second World War, a significant force in labor's post war support for Democratic presidential candidates, and a key advisor to the AFL-CIO's George Meany. Born in Chicago in 1896, he was the eldest of eight children. He left school at an early age to help support his family after his father was injured and he became an electrician by trade. He participated in the labor movement in Chicago, beginning with the International Brotherhood of Electrical Workers' (IBEW) Local 134 in 1914, and then from 1937 as Secretary of the Chicago Federation of Labor. In 1940, he moved to Washington, DC, to work with President Franklin Roosevelt's National Defense Advisory Commission to mobilize national defense in the face of Hitler's European onslaught. He eventually became the Vice Chairman for Labor of the War Production Board, 1943-1945, where he worked effectively to stabilize industrial relations in the construction field and to halt strikes and work stoppages while arbitration agreements were conducted. He served in postwar Germany, 1945-1948, as both an advisor to American commander General Lucius D. Clay and as President Truman's special coordinator between labor and industry for reorganizing trade unions.

Keenan returned home for the 1948 elections where he was first director of the League for Political Education where he was credited with an important role in Truman's upset victory over Thomas Dewey. He later served as labor's campaign liaison with presidents John F. Kennedy (1960) and Lyndon B. Johnson (1964), Vice President Hubert Humphrey (1968), and Senator George McGovern (1972). He served as first Secretary of the Building and Construction Trades Department of the American Federation of Labor (AFL), 1951-1954, and IBEW International Secretary, 1954-1976. He was a key friend and advisor to George Meany when the latter merged the rival AFL and CIO into one organization in 1955 and Keenan served thereafter as Vice President and Executive Council member of the combined AFL-CIO. He was an active Catholic layman and was honored with the papal medal, Pro Ecclesia et Pontifice Award, in 1973, and an honorary doctorate from The Catholic University of America (CUA) in 1974. He was also a recipient of the Presidential Medal of Merit and Presidential Medal of Freedom. He was known to support civil rights organizations and the State of Israel. He was married three times, first to Ethel Fosburg, by which they had son Joseph Jr; second to Mytle Fox, whose son John was adopted by Keenan; and third to Jeffie Hennessy.

Scope and Contents

The Joseph Daniel Keenan Papers (1935-1976) consist of correspondence, memos, minutes, reports, legal documents, articles, news releases, telegrams, newspaper clippings, and photographs relating to Keenan's long and active union career and to his involvement in government and civic affairs. The correspondence series includes material from the early years of Keenan's career when he was Recording and Correspondence Secretary for the Chicago Federation of Labor (1937-1949) and deals primarily with union disputes, especially the unionization of the Kohler Company. Other material pertaining to Keenan's early career can be found in the 1976 oral history interview. The correspondence and the miscellaneous articles, reports, and publications, as well as the 1971 oral history interview, contain material relating to two important positions which Keenan held during world War II and the years immediately following. As a member of the War Production Board (1940-1945) he was in charge of labor production for the war effort, and, after the war, he aided in the reorganization of the German labor unions under General Lucius Clay of the War Manpower Division in Germany (1945-1948). There is little material from the 1950s and early 1960s when Keenan entered on his IBEW international Secretaryship and AFL-CIO Vice Presidency. In the correspondence series, this period is represented by some miscellaneous letters and memos which do not seem to be directly related to either his union or his service activities. There are however several pieces of correspondence mainly in the form of memos, from several prominent political figures of this period, presidents, senators, and presidential aides.

The bulk of the collection is found in the two subject series, the Union Activities Subject files (1956-1976) and the Service Activities Subject Files (1960-1976). Keenan's union activities have been divided into two main groups, the IBEW and the AFL-CIO, and contain material relation to committee work and meetings of international labor organizations at which he represented these organizations. Much of this is printed or duplicated and is dated after 1965. The materials from the various meetings, while primarily in the form of printed agenda items and published minutes and reports, frequently provide information on labor conditions in various areas of the world. Two files of special interest deal with the United Farm Workers and Keenan's special trip to Vietnam. As a member of the Executive Committee on the AFL-CIO, Keenan investigated the troubles between the teamsters and the farm workers over unionization. There is extensive correspondence between Keenan, Cesar Chavez, and Monsignor George G. Higgins, of the United States Catholic Conference, who acted as an intermediary. The other important file documents Keenan's trip to South Vietnam in 1967, under the joint sponsorship of the AFL-CIO and the United States Government, to study South Vietnam's labor movement.

Series 7, photographs, though largely undated, is a good supplement to Keenan's papers. They show the many activities in which he participated. Two groups of particular interest are those from post war Germany and from Keenan's trip to South Vietnam. The numerous pictures of Keenan with political and union officials illustrate the respect and esteem in which he is held by those with whom he works. In addition, Series 8 and 9 are two addendums, which, in effect, make up series eight and nine. These consist of material, much of it photocopied, for Francis X. Gannon's 1984 Keenan biography as well as miscellaneous files donated by Thomas A. Hannigan, Keenan's former Administrative Assistant at the IBEW.

Series 4: Miscellaneous Articles, Reports, and Publications, 1938-1974 (2 boxes)

The bulk of this series is from the 1940s and 1950s and consists of a variety of materials chronologically arranged. There are articles on Keenan and the labor movement, legal documents such as law suits and Congressional legislation, and material related to Keenan's work in post war Germany. Among the miscellaneous items is a signed copy of Cardinal O'Boyle's address when Keenan received an honorary doctorate in 1974. Another item of interest is an 1889 Souvenir Programme" of Chicago's Trade and Labor Assembly which includes an historical essay on the Assembly and a brief history of the American Federation of Labor.

Series 5: Union Activities Subject Files, 1956-1976 (10 boxes)

This series is divided into two main groups of papers, arranged chronologically by subject: those pertaining to the IBEW and those of the AFL-CIO. The IBEW papers include correspondence and conference materials of the International Metalworkers Federation (IMF) and the International Federation of Builders and Wood Workers (IFBWW), several of whose meetings Keenan attended as a representative of the IBEW. The AFL-CIO papers consist of general correspondence and committee materials, as well as material relating to several foreign conferences at which Keenan represented the AFL-CIO. Two significant groups of material within this series pertain to the establishment of the United Farm Workers Union and Keenan's trip to South Vietnam in 1967.

Series 7: Photographs, Scrapbooks, and Miscellaneous, n.d. (6 boxes)

The bulk of this series contains photographs covering Keenan's long labor career. The photos are divided into three groups: photos relating to correspondence, dated photos, and undated photos. Two major groups of photos cover post war Germany and Keenan's 1967 trip to South Vietnam, including pictures of Vice President Nguyen Cao Ky and Ambassador Bunker. Key labor figures included are George Meany, William Schnitzler, Dick Walsh, Frank Fitzsimmons, A. Philip Randolph, Arthur Goldber, and Walter Reuther. Political leaders pictured include Presidents Truman, Kennedy, Johnson and Nixon, as well as Hubert Humphrey, Adlai Stevenson, George McGovern, Stuart Symington, Richard Daley, Martin Kennelly, Carl Albert, and Wayne Morse. There are also two scrapbooks, plaques, various citations, and a sampling of convention badges in this series. One of the scrapbooks (volume 18) is a record, through newspaper clippings, of the controversy after Kennedy suggested Keenan's appointment to the Manpower post of the Department of Defense. The other (volume 19) was made by Keenan's sister and portrays his career (ca. 1940-1970) through newspaper clippings. The citations include the Rerum Novarum Award (1961) and the Pro Ecclesia et Pontifice Award (1973). The box of convention badges is a representative sample of the many badges Keenan collected over the years and includes badges from conventions, both state and national, of the AFL, IBEW, and AFL-CIO, as well as from various departments of the AFL-CIO, such as the Building and Construction Trades.
