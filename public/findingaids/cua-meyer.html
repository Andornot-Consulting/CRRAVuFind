Descriptive Summary

Creator: Robert Theodore Meyer
Title: Robert Theodore Meyer Papers
Dates: 1905-1986
Abstract: Robert Theodore Meyer (1911-1987), a native of Ohio, received an M.A. in classical languages from John Carroll University and a doctorate in classics at Michigan University before coming to The Catholic University of America (CUA) in 1947 to serve as an instructor in German and comparative philology. He became assistant professor, then associate professor, full professor, and finally, in 1953, he became chair of the Celtic Studies Department. He gained the title of Professor Emeritus of the Department of Modern Languages from CUA in 1976 when he retired. This collection is comprised of Meyer’s personal papers, which include his diaries, correspondence, postcards, research notes, and manuscripts of his published works.
Extent: 12.25 linear feet; 22 boxes
Language: English
Identification: 62

Biographical Note

Robert Theodore Meyer was born on August 6, 1911 to Louis and Clara Meyer in Cleveland, Ohio. As a child he attended local Catholic schools and graduated from St. Ignatius High School in 1929, with a focus in Classics. He then attended John Carroll University in Cleveland where he earned his Bachelor's and Master's degrees in Classical Languages and Philosophy, in 1933 and 1934, respectively. Following his graduation, Meyer worked for the Baker Publishing Company while researching on a fellowship in Classics and German Philology at Western Reserve University. In 1937, he married Dorothy E. Deex in Berea, Ohio; they would have four children and remain married until Meyer's death. After four years at Western Reserve, Meyer began his doctoral work at the University of Michigan in 1939, and in 1943 received his PhD in Indo-European Linguistics. His dissertation was a study of the sources for the fourteenth century text "Sonyhurst Medulla." Due to his condition of high myopia, Meyer did not serve during World War II but worked for the engineering department of the King-Seeley Corporation from 1943 to 1946. During the early forties he also received several fellowships at the Linguistics Institute at the University of Michigan and the University of North Carolina. After spending one year as an English Philology instructor at St. Louis University, he became an instructor of Comparative Philology at The Catholic University of America (CUA) in 1947. During his career at CUA, Meyer held the Order of the Hibernians Chair in Celtic Studies from 1953-1976, taking over following the death of James A. Geary in 1953. Meyer was versed in Latin, Greek, Celtic, early Christian writings, German, and Icelandic. He also served as a fellow from 1947-1950 at John Hopkins University where he researched Romance Philology, Sanskrit, and Old Icelandic. He also taught English there from 1948-1949. He spent three summers at the Dublin Institute for Advanced Studies in Celtic Philology, 1953-1955. This is where he published his most important work, "Merugud Uilix Meic Leirtis," in 1958. Meyer also published works on the lives of St. Anthony, St. Athanasius, and St. John Chrysostom. He also lectured at Jesus College, Oxford, in Celtic Studies in 1961, 1965, 1968, 1970, and 1972, as well as a semester at Harvard University in 1970. Meyer was a member of many linguistic organizations including The Linguistic Society of America, The Medieval Academy of America, The Modern Language Association of America, and the American Philological Association. Also a devout Catholic, he was ordained a deacon for the Archdiocese of Washington in 1971, for Campus Ministry at Catholic University. At the age of sixty-five, Meyer retired from Catholic University, and following his retirement he split his time between Washington and Harpers Ferry, West Virginia. He continued to teach one course per semester at CUA and continued his research at CUA and abroad. Meyer died on October 3, 1987 in Swansea, Wales, after a bout of encephalitis. At the time of his death he had been touring Wales following a conference he attended. Meyer was considered one of the leading experts in Celtic Studies and Comparative Philology.

Scope and Contents

The Robert Theodore Meyer Papers are divided into nine series. The first consists of two boxes of Meyer's personal diaries of his daily activities. Most of the information therein is related to research he conducted, as well as meetings and events he attended. The second series contains postcards and notes that were originally contained within his diaries, most from friends of his traveling abroad, as well as ticket stubs and travel materials that he saved from his trips in Europe and around the United States.

The four series following contains various types of correspondence, the first containing four boxes of academic correspondence both with CUA and with other universities with which Meyer was involved. The fourth series contains business correspondence, with three boxes mainly concerning travel arrangements, as well as correspondence with different academic organizations. There is also some correspondence with the Order of the Ancient Hibernians (AOH), which funded the department Meyer chaired. The fifth series contains two boxes of Meyer's personal correspondence, mostly with family and friends from different academic circles and with former classmates from his years at John Carroll University and the University of Michigan. The sixth series contains correspondence concerning Meyer’s research research in addition to his research notes, mostly on Celtic Philology as well as Anglo-Saxon and Medieval texts.

The seventh series contains Meyer's manuscripts of work he published, as well as a few unpublished manuscripts. Most of these manuscripts are on Celtic Philology,but included also is his dissertation and a reprint of his book "Megund Uilix Meic Leirtis."

The eighth series has financial records, which includes receipts, banking forms, and correspondences.

The final series has three boxes of publications that include personal publications and clippings he collected throughout the years, as well as a few books and pamphlets on philological writings. Overall, the Robert Theodore Meyer Papers is a useful collection for any researcher doing work on Celtic Studies or philology.
